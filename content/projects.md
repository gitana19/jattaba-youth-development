## Primary Health Care

For many years, the old primary health care building stood still, leaving the community without access to essential medical services. Recognizing the critical need for basic healthcare, JYDA decided in 2017 to revitalize this vital facility. Through community effort and donor support, we renovated the building and reopened it to provide primary medical care and medication distribution to our community members.

## Women Garden

In 2016, the women's garden faced a severe water shortage as the old well could no longer provide sufficient water for irrigation. Understanding the importance of this garden for women's economic empowerment and food security, JYDA mobilized community donations and installed a new water tank system. Additionally, we constructed a new fence around the garden to protect the crops. The photovoltaic modules were installed through a collaborative partnership with Project XYZ, ensuring sustainable and reliable water supply for the garden.

## Women Market

For many years, women in Jattaba sold their goods on the ground under a mango tree, which was neither hygienic nor healthy for the vendors. Recognizing the need for a proper market infrastructure, JYDA decided in 2019 to construct a market hall with individual stalls. After years of fundraising and construction, the market hall was completed in January 2026, providing women vendors with a clean, safe, and dignified space to conduct their business. The facility will also be integrated with a cold storage room and toilet facilities to further improve hygiene and preserve perishable goods.

## Milling Machine

The old milling machines, originally commissioned during the first republic in the 1980s, had fallen into disrepair, leaving the community without a reliable means to process their grain harvests. In response to this critical need, JYDA constructed a new building to house modern milling equipment. Through a joint project with NEMA, launched in 2019 by the former agriculture minister Hon. Amie Fabureh, the facility was established to mill grains and couscous. Community members can now bring their own harvests to be processed at the center. Additionally, women were trained in milling operations, empowering them with valuable skills and creating new economic opportunities.
